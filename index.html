Unlearning for leaving ignorance

22:18 | April 12, 2026

The more we learn, the more powerful we feel. That belief quietly drives almost every human effort, from childhood curiosity to lifelong ambition. We are taught, directly and indirectly, that gathering knowledge is the path to becoming better, wiser, and more complete. At a closer level, this idea seems undeniably true. Knowledge sharpens our thinking, improves our decisions, and helps us build a life that feels meaningful. But when we begin to widen our view, not just to the span of life but beyond it, uncomfortable questions begin to arise. What happens to all that we have learned once life itself comes to an end? Do our thoughts, our understanding, and our carefully built knowledge simply disappear with the body? And what about the state before birth, where none of this existed for us at all?

These questions are not easy to answer, yet they reveal something important. Perhaps the problem is not knowledge itself, but the direction in which we seek it. We often pursue knowledge without asking where it leads or whether it truly serves us. This limitation is a strange kind of ignorance, one that exists even in the most intellectually capable beings on this planet. Humans, despite being the most advanced in terms of thinking and reasoning, often restrict their understanding to narrow paths, unaware of the larger picture.

So what exactly is wrong with this ignorance? At first glance, it does not appear harmful. After all, progress in science, technology, and society is built on the accumulation of knowledge. However, the issue lies in imbalance. When knowledge grows without wisdom, it begins to lose its purpose. In ancient times, this distinction was clearly understood. The rishis were not just seekers of information, but seekers of truth. They possessed the clarity to decide how much knowledge was sufficient for a meaningful life. They understood that beyond a certain point, excess knowledge could become as limiting as too little knowledge.

Centuries ago, Acharya Shankara spoke about the idea that everything in existence has a boundary. According to his vision, both lack of knowledge and excessive knowledge can lead a person into confusion. When knowledge is too little, we remain unaware. When it is too much without direction, we become lost in complexity. In both cases, the result is a form of ignorance that affects not only the present but also the future. A life built on such imbalance cannot sustain itself in a healthy way.

In contrast, the structure of modern education has taken a very different path. Today, learning is heavily centered around material success and measurable outcomes. We are trained to acquire skills that help us earn, compete, and survive in a fast moving world. This system has achieved remarkable success in creating efficiency and innovation. Yet, in doing so, it has quietly pushed aside other equally important dimensions of life. Moral understanding and spiritual awareness are often treated as secondary or even irrelevant. Over time, this creates a gap within individuals, a sense that something essential is missing despite having access to vast amounts of information.

Another challenge arises from how society responds to those who question this imbalance. Anyone who attempts to explore knowledge beyond the material level is often misunderstood. Such individuals may be labeled as impractical, strange, or detached from reality. In some cases, they are quickly categorized under titles like spiritual leaders or dismissed altogether. This reaction discourages deeper inquiry and reinforces the idea that only material knowledge is valuable.

A complete human life, however, cannot be built on a single dimension. Physical well being, mental clarity, and spiritual depth are not separate paths but interconnected aspects of existence. Ignoring any one of them weakens the whole. In the present world, material concerns dominate most priorities. Mental health has begun to receive attention, which is a positive shift, but the spiritual dimension remains largely neglected. One reason for this neglect is the way spirituality is often approached. Instead of thoughtful understanding, many people either reject it entirely or accept it blindly without questioning. Both approaches fail to capture its true essence.

There is an old saying that advises us not to search for the origins of certain things, such as the beginnings of rivers or the lineage of rishis. In a similar way, the deeper roots of this balanced knowledge are not easily traced. They come from a time when material concerns were minimal, mental awareness was stronger, and spiritual understanding held the highest place. If we try to compare modern scientists with ancient rishis using only present standards, we might conclude that modern thinkers are more intelligent. But this conclusion itself may arise from a limited perspective.

The real issue is not a lack of intelligence, but the presence of multiple layers of ignorance. We often lack understanding of ourselves, of others, and of the world around us. We struggle to see the difference between what is truly important and what is temporary. There is confusion in our sense of morality, in our ability to reason deeply, and in our capacity for selflessness. When such forms of ignorance accumulate, they create a path that could eventually harm the very existence of humanity.

If this is the situation, then what can be done to move in a better direction? The answer may not lie in acquiring more knowledge, but in learning to let go of certain kinds of knowledge. Not everything we learn is necessary, and not everything we know adds value to our lives. Unlearning becomes just as important as learning. By stepping back from excessive material focus and allowing space for deeper reflection, we begin to see beyond immediate gains.

Traditionally, guidance from a teacher or a guru played a crucial role in this process. Such guidance helped individuals navigate the complexities of knowledge and avoid confusion. In the present time, finding such guidance has become extremely difficult. Trust between people has weakened, and genuine mentorship is rare. Yet, the importance of guidance remains unchanged. Acharya Vishnugupta once pointed out that knowledge gained without proper guidance is like a precious gem placed in the hands of a monkey. It may be valuable, but without understanding, it cannot be used meaningfully.

When ignorance continues unchecked, it quietly takes away the sense of fulfillment in life. People may achieve success, accumulate wealth, and gain recognition, yet still feel incomplete. This lack of fulfillment is not due to a shortage of knowledge, but due to its imbalance. True contentment comes from aligning knowledge with wisdom, and from recognizing that not everything worth knowing can be measured or stored.

In the end, the question is not how much we know, but how well we understand the purpose of what we know. Only when knowledge is guided by wisdom and balanced across all aspects of life can it truly lead to something meaningful.
